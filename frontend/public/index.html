üí° Understanding Percentiles

What is a percentile? A percentile tells you what percentage of people earn less than a given amount.

Example: The 25th percentile (P25) is $16,900. This means 25% of Canadians earn less than $16,900, and 75% earn more.

The median (P50) is $37,358. This is the middle point where exactly 50% of people earn less and 50% earn more. It's the most common way to describe "typical" income.

‚ùì Frequently Asked Questions

Where does this data come from?
This data comes from Statistics Canada's Census of Population 2021, which is the most comprehensive and reliable source for income distribution in Canada. The census surveys millions of Canadians and provides official government statistics on employment income.
How current is this data?
The income data reflects the 2020 calendar year (collected during the 2021 Census). While this is the most recent comprehensive census data available, please note that income levels and distributions may have changed since 2020 due to inflation, economic growth, and other factors.
What does "employment income" include?
Employment income includes wages, salaries, commissions, and net self-employment income from both farm and non-farm businesses. It does not include investment income, rental income, pensions, or government transfers. This is your gross (before-tax) income from work.
Why isn't age-specific data shown in the comparison?
Currently, the tool uses Canada-wide data for all ages combined. While the Census does provide age-specific income breakdowns (15-24, 25-34, 35-44, etc.), we collect your age for potential future features that will allow you to compare your income against others in your age group. This would give you a more relevant comparison since income typically increases with age and career progression.
Why not use the annual household income survey instead?
While Statistics Canada does publish annual household income data (such as Table 36-10-0587), these surveys measure household-level income rather than individual earnings. They also use broader economic concepts like "compensation" that include employer benefits and contributions, which don't match what people think of as their "salary." The Census provides individual employment income data with detailed percentiles, making it much better for personal comparisons. The trade-off is that Census data is only updated every 5 years, while household surveys are annual.
